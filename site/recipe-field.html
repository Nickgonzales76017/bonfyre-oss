The field research problem

Why cloud tools break for serious field work.

No connectivity

Cloud tools require the cloud to be there

Oral history interviews, ethnographic recordings, archaeological site audio, remote field notes — this work happens where there's no internet. Cloud transcription and cloud search require connectivity at processing time. Bonfyre runs everything on the laptop in your bag.

Volume and cost

200 field recordings at $0.006/minute = $200+ just to transcribe

A week of field work produces dozens of recordings. At Deepgram's standard rate, transcribing 200 × 30-minute recordings costs roughly $360. BonfyreTranscribe costs nothing per recording after the hardware. A single local run processes all 200 overnight without an API key.

Findability

File systems don't answer research questions

A folder of 200 recordings labelled by date and location answers "what did I record on Tuesday" — it doesn't answer "all interviews where the subject mentioned the 1973 drought." BonfyreVec + BonfyreQuery give you semantic search across the entire archive.

Worked example

A week of oral history interviews, processed and published.

The scenario: 15 oral history interviews recorded during a week-long field trip, no internet access. You return to the office. Here's exactly what happens.

Step 1 — BonfyreIngest (batch)

15 recordings normalised and fingerprinted overnight

BonfyrePipeline queues all 15 recordings. BonfyreIngest processes each: normalise loudness, resample to 16 kHz mono, trim silence, store BLAKE2b hash. This runs overnight while you sleep. If you add 5 more recordings next week and re-run, only the 5 new files are processed — the existing 15 hash to the same values and are skipped.

Step 2 — BonfyreTranscribe (batch)

15 × 45-minute interviews → transcripts, no internet

BonfyreTranscribe processes each recording with your local FPQ-compressed Whisper model. No internet. No API key. At 15× faster than real-time, 15 × 45 minutes of audio takes about 45 minutes of compute on M2 Pro. All 15 transcripts produced before morning.

Step 3 — BonfyreSegment + BonfyreTranscriptClean

Interviewer and subject labelled, fillers removed

Each transcript goes through speaker diarisation (interviewer voice vs. subject voice) and Hyperscan pattern cleaning. Hallucinations and fillers removed in under 30 ms per transcript. The subject's voice — the historically significant content — is now cleanly separated from the researcher's questions.

Step 4 — BonfyreParagraph + BonfyreBrief

Each interview becomes a structured record

Paragraph groups each transcript into thematic sections: "Early Life", "Key Events", "Community Memory", "Personal Reflection". BonfyreBrief extracts a structured summary for each interview: subject biography, key dates mentioned, themes, notable quotes, and recommended follow-up questions for a return visit.

Step 5 — BonfyreTag + BonfyreCanon

Named entities extracted and resolved across all 15 interviews

BonfyreTag extracts named entities from every brief: people, places, dates, events, organisations. BonfyreCanon runs across the full batch — if 7 of the 15 interviews mention "the old mill" and "the Miller property" and "that building on Route 9," Canon resolves these to a single entity. Cross-interview connections become visible.

Step 6 — BonfyreTranscriptFamily

15 interviews clustered into a searchable family

BonfyreTranscriptFamily analyses the 15 transcripts as a collection, grouping interviews by shared entities, overlapping themes, and referenced events. The output is a family structure: this interview is related to these 4 others, those 3 form a cluster around the 1973 flood. The family structure becomes the navigation backbone of the published archive.

Step 7 — BonfyreEmbed + BonfyreVec + BonfyreIndex

All 15 briefs indexed for semantic search

BonfyreEmbed generates sentence vectors for every brief. BonfyreVec stores them in a zero-copy LMDB index. BonfyreIndex builds full-text search alongside it. Together these let you ask: "all interviews where the subject mentioned farming practices before 1960" — and get back the 4 relevant interviews ranked by semantic relevance, not keyword frequency.

Step 8 — BonfyreRender + BonfyreEmit

A browsable archive site generated from the family structure

BonfyreRender builds an HTML archive: a landing page with the family cluster view, individual pages per interview with the structured brief, related-interview links, and the full transcript. BonfyreEmit produces a JSON export of the full archive — for importing into other research tools — and a PDF packet per interview for printing or sharing.

Step 9 — BonfyrePack + BonfyreProof

Archive bundle signed and preserved

BonfyrePack assembles the entire archive — all transcripts, briefs, HTML pages, audio files, JSON export — into a content-addressed bundle. BonfyreProof signs it with Ed25519. The bundle ID is a stable identifier for this exact state of the archive. Future deposits to an institutional repository can reference this bundle ID as the version deposited.

Step 10 — BonfyreDistribute (lossy sync)

Synced to institutional server even over flaky field internet

BonfyreDistribute delivers the pack using fountain codes over QUIC. If you're syncing from a field site with 40% packet loss, the transfer still completes — fountain codes make any subset of received chunks sufficient to reconstruct the full pack. The archive is preserved at the institution without requiring a stable connection to finish the transfer.

All 15 interviews processed, indexed, and published without a single API call or cloud dependency.
