Pipeline recipe · Education & archives

Lectures and oral histories → public memory.

A recorded lecture or oral history interview holds knowledge that shouldn't live only in one person's Google Drive. Bonfyre processes it into structured notes, a semantic search index, and a publish-ready static site — so it becomes findable, browsable, and shareable institutional memory.

The typical educational archive — and its friction.

YouTube + docs

Worked example

A 12-lecture course series converted to a searchable public archive.

The scenario: a professor records 12 lectures over a semester, each 50 minutes. They want the lectures accessible to future students as structured notes and a searchable archive, not just a playlist of videos.

Step 1 — BonfyreIngest (batch)

12 lecture recordings normalised overnight

BonfyrePipeline queues all 12 recordings. BonfyreIngest normalises each: loudness, 16 kHz mono, silence trim, BLAKE2b hash. New lectures added at the start of the next semester will only be processed if their hash is new — existing lectures are skipped. The archive is incremental.

Step 2 — BonfyreTranscribe (batch)

12 × 50-minute lectures → transcripts in under an hour

Local Whisper at 15× real-time processes the full 10-hour lecture series in approximately 40 minutes. No API key. No $900 Rev.com bill. The professor runs this overnight and has all 12 transcripts by morning.

Step 3 — BonfyreTranscriptClean

Hallucinations and repeated fillers removed from all 12

Hyperscan pattern matching removes false transcription artifacts and repeated verbal tics in under 30 ms per transcript. The clean transcripts are what students will read — they don't contain the noise that makes raw Whisper output hard to follow.

Step 4 — BonfyreParagraph

Each lecture grouped into topical sections

BonfyreParagraph groups each lecture's transcript into thematic sections: "Introduction and context", "Core concept", "Case study", "Questions raised." Each section gets a header. The lecture is now structured content, not a wall of speech.

Step 5 — BonfyreBrief

Structured lecture notes extracted from each transcript

BonfyreBrief produces lecture notes per session: key concepts introduced, definitions given, examples used, questions posed, references cited, and suggested readings. These are the structured notes a student would produce manually from attending — generated automatically from the transcript.

Step 6 — BonfyreTag + BonfyreCanon

Concepts and people named across all 12 lectures

BonfyreTag extracts named concepts, theorists, works cited, and events across all 12 lectures. BonfyreCanon resolves across the batch — if "the tragedy of the commons" is referenced in 4 lectures, Canon creates a single entity node linking all 4 references. Students can navigate by concept, not just by lecture number.

Step 7 — BonfyreTranscriptFamily

12 lectures organised into a thematic curriculum structure

BonfyreTranscriptFamily analyses the 12 transcripts as a collection: which lectures form thematic clusters, which build on each other, which share primary sources. The output is a curriculum graph — the navigation backbone of the published course site.

Step 8 — BonfyreEmbed + BonfyreVec + BonfyreIndex

Full semantic search across the course

BonfyreEmbed vectorises all 12 briefs. BonfyreVec stores them in LMDB. BonfyreIndex builds full-text alongside. Students can now ask "explain the lecture that covered collective action problems" and get the relevant lecture, the relevant section, and related lectures — not just a keyword match.

Step 9 — BonfyreRender + BonfyreEmit

Static course site generated: per-lecture pages, search, curriculum map

BonfyreRender generates the complete course site: a landing page with the curriculum graph, individual lecture pages with structured notes and clean transcript, a concept index, and cross-lecture navigation. BonfyreEmit produces a JSON export of the full archive for institutional deposit or import into another system.

Step 10 — BonfyreSync

Course site published to GitHub Pages or internal server

BonfyreSync pushes the generated site to its target host. Public courses go to GitHub Pages — free, fast, no server to maintain. Restricted institutional archives go to an internal server with access controls. The same pipeline, different sync target.

A 10-hour lecture series → structured notes, concept index, searchable archive, and published course site. Processed on local hardware in under an hour.

What you end up with

After the pipeline runs.

Searchable course site

Students find what they need by meaning, not lecture number

BonfyreEmbed + BonfyreVec give the course site semantic search. A student preparing an essay asks "which lecture discussed Ostrom's work on resource governance" and gets the answer immediately — not "watch all 12 lectures and find it yourself."

Structured lecture notes

Auto-generated notes students actually want to read

BonfyreBrief produces the structured-notes output students would pay a note-service to generate. Key concepts, definitions, examples, questions. Generated automatically. Available alongside the full transcript for students who want the full record.

Durable institutional memory

Lives on GitHub Pages for free, no CMS to maintain

Static HTML files. No CMS. No server. No SaaS subscription. The course archive exists as a folder of files that can be hosted anywhere for free. Oral history museums, public curricula, research documentation — built once, maintained forever.
