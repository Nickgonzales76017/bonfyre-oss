Industry Recipes

What you already have → what you get. Each recipe maps your existing inputs to a Bonfyre pipeline and a concrete output. No new tools to learn — just a different place for the work to happen.

Eight industries

Input · pipeline · output.

Property management

Voicemail → maintenance packet

Resident leaves a voicemail → BonfyreTranscribe → BonfyreBrief extracts issue type and follow-up items → BonfyrePack bundles a vendor-ready maintenance packet → BonfyreSync delivers to your system.

Input: voicemail  ·  Output: structured maintenance ticket + vendor-ready PDF bundle

What they share

Same infrastructure, different outputs.

The input

Raw audio or text is always the starting point

Every recipe starts with something you already create: a call, a recording, a voicemail, a meeting. You don't change how you work — you add a pipeline that processes what you already generate. The pipeline runs after the meeting is over; it doesn't interrupt the meeting.

The processing

Transcribe → structure → format → distribute

Every recipe follows the same four-stage arc. Transcription (BonfyreTranscribe + cleanup) → structuring (BonfyreBrief + context-specific extraction) → formatting (BonfyreRender + BonfyreEmit for the right surface) → distribution (BonfyrePack + BonfyreSync or BonfyreDistribute). The binaries swap; the architecture doesn't.

The output

Something you can hand someone

Every recipe produces a tangible deliverable — not a log file, not a dashboard metric, not a database row. A PDF, an HTML page, a structured bundle, a CRM entry, a signed proof. Infrastructure becomes a product when the output is something a client, a vendor, a reviewer, or a regulator can open and act on.

Privacy by default

Nothing leaves your infrastructure

Every recipe above runs entirely on-device or on your own server. Legal intake calls never transit a SaaS API. Medical recordings stay on-premise. Incident war rooms don't get logged by a third-party transcription service. Local processing isn't a configuration option — it's the default.
